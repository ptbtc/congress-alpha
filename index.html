Section 01

Executive Summary

Key Finding

Copying congressional trades at disclosure generates negative alpha. Mean 30d excess return: −0.74% (p < 0.001). The underperformance worsens with horizon.

We scraped 4,512 trades from Capitol Trades, which aggregates from US House & Senate Periodic Transaction Report (PTR) filings under the STOCK Act. After deduplication and filtering to stock trades with valid tickers, 3,962 trades remained across 71 politicians and 793 tickers, with a data collection cutoff of March 31, 2026. Trades disclosed after September 2025 were excluded from the return analysis because they lack sufficient forward price history (30–180 days) for meaningful return calculation. All prices were fetched from Yahoo Finance.

The finding: copying congressional trades at disclosure does not generate alpha. The mean 30-day excess return is −0.74% (t = −5.18, p < 0.001), with only 43.5% of trades beating SPY. The underperformance worsens with horizon: −1.37% at 60 days, −1.84% at 90 days. Every horizon is statistically significant at the 1% level.

The signal is concentrated in a handful of individuals. Boozman (+2.91% at 30d, 62% win rate, 52 trades) and Tuberville (+4.71%, 54% win rate, 26 trades) show positive alpha. Tim Moore leads with +9.27% across 15 trades. But these are exceptions — the aggregate is decisively negative, and most high-volume traders (Ro Khanna with 1,297 trades at −1.82%, McCaul with 470 at −1.73%) drag the mean down.

Implication: the "Pelosi tracker" narrative is noise. Sector-relative analysis reveals that congressional members tilt heavily toward technology — their apparent outperformance in earlier, smaller studies was beta to QQQ, not stock-selection skill. When benchmarked against sector ETFs rather than SPY, even the tech-heavy portfolios show no edge. Consumer staples is the worst sector (−1.41% excess vs XLP at 30d, p < 0.001). The copycat trade is dead.

Section 02

Data & Methodology

Pipeline Summary

  • Source: Capitol Trades (capitoltrades.com), operated by 2iQ Research — aggregates House & Senate PTR filings
  • Scope: Stock trades only (excludes ETFs, bonds, mutual funds, options, crypto)
  • Sample: 4,512 scraped → 4,077 deduplicated → 3,962 with valid price data
  • Prices: Yahoo Finance (yfinance), adjusted closes, auto_adjust=True
  • Benchmark: SPY (broad), sector ETFs (XLK, XLV, XLF, XLE, XLY, XLP, XLI, XLB, XLU, XLRE, XLC)
  • Horizons: 30, 60, 90, 180 calendar days from disclosure date
  • Failed tickers: 43 excluded (delistings, acquisitions, ticker changes)
  • Statistical test: One-sample t-test, H₀: mean excess return = 0

Returns are computed from the disclosure date — the date Capitol Trades publishes the filing — not the trade date. This is the earliest date a copycat investor could act. When a target date falls on a weekend or holiday, the next available trading day's close is used.

For sell trades, excess returns are calculated identically to buys (forward return from disclosure). Trade values use STOCK Act range midpoints. No transaction costs, slippage, or portfolio construction rules are modeled.

Section 06

Discussion

Why negative alpha? The most likely explanation is adverse selection: by the time a trade is disclosed (mean lag: ~25 days), the information advantage has fully dissipated. Worse, disclosure itself creates a crowded trade — platforms like Quiver Quantitative, Capitol Trades, and Unusual Whales push alerts to millions of retail investors. The resulting buying pressure at disclosure inflates entry prices for copycats, creating a systematic disadvantage. The worsening performance at longer horizons (−0.74% → −1.84% at 90d) suggests mean reversion after the initial disclosure spike.

Survivorship bias in the narrative: Earlier, smaller studies — including our own v1 (68 trades, +4.02% excess) — suffered from selection bias. High-profile trades that worked get media coverage and enter datasets; quiet losers don't. When we expand to the full Capitol Trades universe (3,962 trades), the selection bias vanishes and the alpha turns negative. The "Pelosi tracker" meme persists because of availability bias: her TSLA and AVGO wins are memorable; her RBLX, PANW, and DIS losses are not.

Sector beta confusion: Congressional portfolios are heavily tilted toward technology (894 of 3,962 trades = 22.6%). During the 2023–2024 AI boom, even random tech stock picks outperformed SPY — not because of information advantage, but because of sector beta. When we benchmark against XLK instead of SPY, tech trades show −0.83% excess at 30d. The apparent alpha was QQQ exposure masquerading as stock-picking skill. Energy is the one sector where Congress genuinely outperforms the sector benchmark (+1.22% vs XLE, p = 0.03) — potentially reflecting actual committee-linked information.

Section 07

Conclusion

Bottom Line

Don't copy Congress. Sector ETFs outperform congressional stock picks. Individual exceptions exist but are not reliably tradeable in advance.

For investors: the copycat trade is negative-expectancy after a proper sample size. If you want tech exposure, buy XLK. If you want energy exposure, buy XLE. Both outperform congressional members' individual stock picks in their respective sectors. The few individuals with positive alpha (Boozman, Tuberville, Tim Moore) have small samples, and there is no reliable way to identify future winners ex ante.

For regulators: the STOCK Act's 45-day disclosure window is largely irrelevant — the data shows no copycat edge to eliminate. However, the insider perspective (trade-date returns) remains untested at this scale because we cannot observe the counterfactual. The $200 fine for late filing remains inadequate: Scott Franklin's average reporting lag is 305 days, Byron Donalds' is 322 days, Val Hoyle's is 234 days.

For researchers: future work should test whether a filtered strategy (e.g., Senate-only, energy-committee-only, first-30-days-after-legislation) can isolate positive alpha within the broader negative signal. Our data hints at this: Senate trades show +1.88% excess at 30d (n=203, p=0.023), and energy trades beat XLE. The alpha may exist, but it's not where the retail crowd is looking.

Section 08

Key Literature

The case for alpha: Ziobrowski et al. (2004) found Senate portfolios beat the market by ~85 bps/month (~12% annualized) using 1993–1998 data. The 2011 House extension showed ~6% annualized. Critically, both studies used trade dates, not disclosure dates — the documented alpha was never available to outside investors. Hall, Karadas & Schlosky (2017) showed new members earned ~13.6% annualized alpha after taking office but none before, supporting the information-advantage hypothesis.

The case against: Eggers & Hainmueller (2013) found no significant abnormal returns in 2004–2008 data with more rigorous methods, attributing earlier findings to small samples and methodological choices. Karadas (2019) found returns disappeared post-STOCK Act, suggesting the transparency requirement had a genuine deterrent effect.

The nuance: Li, Michelson, Mollica & Zhou (2025) used NLP to identify "enterprising trades" — those executed 1–30 days before legislative events. These specific trades earned significantly higher returns, suggesting information advantage exists but is concentrated in committee-linked legislative timing, not broad copycat strategies. Our 3,962-trade dataset confirms this: the aggregate is negative, but a few committee-connected individuals show persistent alpha.
